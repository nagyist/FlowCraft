## What is a Sankey diagram?

A Sankey diagram is a flow visualization where the width of each arrow is proportional to the quantity it represents. Flows move from sources on one side to destinations on the other, so you can see at a glance how a total is split, combined, or redistributed.

Sankey diagrams are strongest when the story is about proportions. Energy use, budget allocation, user acquisition funnels, material flows, and supply chains are all easier to understand as weighted flows than as tables of numbers, because a Sankey puts magnitude and direction in the same picture.

## Who uses Sankey diagrams?

- Energy analysts and sustainability researchers visualizing consumption and loss
- Finance and operations teams showing how revenue splits into cost categories
- Product analysts mapping user acquisition, activation, and churn volumes
- Data scientists explaining multi-stage funnels and conversion rates
- Supply chain and logistics planners tracking material or shipment flows
- Policy researchers presenting public spending, tax, or resource allocation
- Educators teaching conservation of mass, energy, or other flow-based concepts

## FAQ

### What kinds of data work best in a Sankey diagram?

Sankey diagrams shine when you have a single quantity that splits, merges, or transforms across stages. Energy flow, budget allocation, user funnels, time spent by activity, and supply chain throughput are all strong fits. If your data is purely categorical with no magnitudes, a different diagram type may be clearer.

### Do I need to provide exact numbers?

No. You can describe the flows qualitatively, such as "most traffic goes to search, a smaller share to social," and the AI will pick reasonable weights. For precise diagrams, include the percentages or raw values in your prompt so the widths reflect real data.
